MLB Salary Analysis¶

In the following analysis, I will delve deeper into the age-old debate whether big spending MLB teams win more games than their lower spending competitors.

The economics of MLB has changed over the course of history with certain structural changes and events shaping the game as it exists today; including the addition of more teams (30 today vs. 24 in 1976), free agency (1976), the ownership collusion scandal (1985-1987), and the Collective Bargaining Agreement (2006).

In 2001 and 2002 “Moneyball” was introduced into the game by the Oakland Athletics which utilized big data structures and statistical analysis that re-wrote how risk and value, as applied to prospects, was modeled and optimized. One question that is always being asked is "Are we paying too much for each win?"

Predictably, MLB team salaries have increased markedly since 1985, the period I will analyze. In-turn, cost-per-win has increased with expanding payrolls. To determine if cost-per-win is statistically significant in predicting team wins and playoff appearances, I will seek to answer the following three questions:

1) Is there a correlation between team salary & wins?

2) Is there a correlation between team salary and making the playoffs?

3) Is there a clear gap between the spending of teams that made the playoffs vs. teams that didn't make the playoffs?

I anticipate the visualization of data to expose several outliers analyzing the payrolls of big market teams (New York Yankees, Boston Red Sox) versus mid-market teams (Kansas City Royals, Baltimore Orioles), and small-market teams (Miami Marlins, Houston Astros). The sample sizes of teams making the playoffs versus teams that do not make the playoffs (10 vs. 20 in 2015) may also affect the outcome.

Hypothesis¶

Hypothesis: The more a team spends, the likelihood of winning games and making the playoffs increases.

Independent variable: salary

Dependent variables: wins, playoff appearances

Correlation Coefficient¶

I want to calculate the correlation coefficient to measure strength and direction of the linear relationship between salary and wins.

Correlation coefficient: a number between −1 and +1 calculated to represent the linear dependence of two variables or sets of data.

Solving for Pearson's r, the covariance of the variables divided by the product of their standard deviations strength, the calculation shows an overall relationship between salary and wins; less strong between 1985 - 1995, although correlation is there, and stronger in the late 90's and early 00's.

P-value test¶

The next question I have is whether the above correlation is statistically significant. To determine this I will calculate a p-value test, which will determines if the null hypothesis holds true or does not.

The null hypothesis, in this case, states there is no relationship between salary/wins:

if p-value > .05, we accept the null hypothesis & determine there is no significance

if p-value < .05, we reject the null hypothesis & determine there is significant relationship

The result when calculating the p-value below is 3.47 X 10-264, which is very close to zero. This number indicates a strong correlation between salary and wins; and therefore we reject the null hypothesis.

Results¶

The period from 1985 – 1995 saw relative parity for MLB teams in which wins and salary are closely correlated. The years between 1995 – 2010 show an sustainable increase in positive correlation, which peaks in the late 90’s and mid-2000’s. From the 2010 – 2015 correlation settles back into the range seen in the mid-80’s and the league reverts to a more competitive pattern in terms of spending to achieve wins.

def correlation(x,y):
    std_x=(x-x.mean())/x.std(ddof=0)
    std_y=(y-y.mean())/y.std(ddof=0)

    return (std_x * std_y).mean()

From 1985 through 1991 parity was evident and team spending did not factor into whether a team made the playoffs, as it did so in later years. Additionally, there was the ownership collusion scandal ('85 - '87) which maintained the parity of teams.

Beginning in the mid-1990's, with additional revenue from television contracts and merchandising contracts, there is a growing gap in what teams were spending, indeed able to spend, and chances of higher spending teams making the playoffs.

Extreme divergence is seen in 2004 where two of the highest spending teams, Red Sox and Yankess with a combined payroll of $300 million, attributed to the huge gap between playoff teams salaries and non-playoff team salaries. Combined, the Yankees and Red Sox payroll was more than the 8 lowest team payrolls combined!

From 2010 we see a reversion to parity. Reasons could be the introduction of wild card games in 2011. Any outliers like the Yankees or Red Sox of 2004 will have a lesser effect on the mean of a a 10 playoff team post-season when compared to an 8 team playoff postseason.

Conclusion¶

To conclude, the analysis shows that wins and salaries are closely related and that there is an overall strong correlation between teams that spend more and win more, and teams that spend more and make the playoffs. However, certain events over time have had their impact on this correlation. Due to randomness, structural changes like television contracts, media rights deals, free agency, collective bargaining agreement, and the ownership collusion scandal there has been varying degrees of correlation over the years. Certain changes like expanded playoff structure and wild card teams have brought more parity to the game today, giving small market teams a greater chance of making the playoffs.
